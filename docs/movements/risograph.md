# Risograph

**Period:** 1986–present

## Overview

The Risograph is a digital duplicator introduced by the Riso Kagaku Corporation of Japan in 1986. It was designed for high-volume, low-cost office printing — schools, churches, political leaflets — but from the early 2000s it was adopted by independent publishers, zinesters and illustrators as an affordable, tactile, small-run printing technology. Studios such as Risotto (Glasgow), Colorama (Berlin) and Perfectly Acceptable (Chicago) made Riso central to contemporary indie print culture. The look combines the visual grammar of screen-printing (flat spot colours, misregistration) with the speed of photocopy.

## Technique

- the artwork is separated into individual spot-colour layers, one per pass
- each layer burns a thermal stencil ("master") wrapped around an ink drum
- soy-based ink is forced through the master onto the paper by a rotating drum
- each colour is printed on a separate pass; paper is reloaded between passes
- registration drifts slightly between passes, producing offset colour edges
- only solid soy-ink spot colours are available — tones are simulated with coarse halftone dots

## Visual signatures for AI

- limited palette of 2–4 bright, slightly chalky spot colours (fluorescent pink, federal blue, yellow, teal)
- visible overprints where two spot colours cross: pink + blue = purple patches
- coarse, organic halftone grain; each dot slightly irregular
- 1–3 mm registration offset, so coloured outlines sit proudly of line art
- patchy ink coverage in solids ("roller marks", faint streaks)
- uncoated, off-white paper stock showing through
- show-through or ghosting from the previous page in a booklet
