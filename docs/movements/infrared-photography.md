# Infrared Photography (Kodak Aerochrome)

**Period:** 1942–present

## Overview

Colour infrared photography originated with Kodak Aerochrome, a false-colour reversal film developed during the Second World War for camouflage detection: living foliage reflects near-infrared strongly while painted nets do not. Aerochrome and its successors were used in military reconnaissance, vegetation surveys and geological mapping. After Kodak discontinued Aerochrome III (EIR) in 2009, the aesthetic was kept alive by contemporary artists — most visibly Richard Mosse, whose Congo series *Infra* and *The Enclave* brought the magenta-foliage palette into fine-art and photojournalism.

## Technique

- three emulsion layers sensitised to green, red and near-infrared (rather than blue, green, red)
- a yellow (minus-blue) filter is required over the lens, otherwise blue light fogs all three layers
- dyes are shifted one channel: IR prints as red, red prints as green, green prints as blue
- living chlorophyll reflects strongly in near-IR and therefore renders as vivid red, pink or magenta
- water and shadows fall very dark; skies and distant haze render cyan
- skin takes a waxy, slightly greenish tone because human tissue has moderate IR reflectance

## Visual signatures for AI

- foliage is pink / magenta / crimson, not green
- clear blue skies render cyan-to-petrol
- tarmac and water are nearly black; white clouds stay white
- skin has a waxy pale-green cast, lips look muted
- painted surfaces keep approximately their normal colour — the "camouflage-detection" signature
- slight halation around highlights typical of Aerochrome III (EIR)
