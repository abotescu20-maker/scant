# WPA Poster / Federal Art Project

**Period:** 1935-1943 (New Deal United States)

## Overview

The Federal Art Project (FAP) was a division of the Works Progress Administration, established in 1935 to employ artists during the Great Depression. Its Poster Division, run largely out of New York, produced roughly 35,000 designs and an estimated two million printed posters promoting public health, national parks, WPA theatre, adult education, travel and civic safety. The surviving archive (about 900 designs) is held by the Library of Congress.

## Technique

- **Silkscreen (serigraphy)** was the dominant printing method — cheap, flat, saturated colour in small editions
- Flat colour planes, minimal gradients, strong black outlines
- Typography integrated with image, often hand-drawn
- Bold sans-serif and geometric display lettering; occasional slab-serifs
- Limited palette (often 3-5 inks) driven by silkscreen economics
- Simplified, near-geometric figures — influenced by European [[constructivism|Constructivism]], [[art-deco|Art Deco]] and the Bauhaus poster school

## Visual signatures for AI

- Flat areas of matte colour with crisp screened edges
- "See America" travel poster tropes: stylised national parks, industrial skylines
- Public-health messaging layout: big headline, small supporting line, single illustration
- 1930s-1940s American modernism palette — muted reds, mustards, teals, cream paper
- Slight mis-registration between colour layers (a signature of hand silkscreen)
