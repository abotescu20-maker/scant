# Russian Constructivism

**Period:** c. 1915-1934 (peak 1920s)

## Overview

An artistic and architectural movement originating in revolutionary Russia, rejecting "art for art's sake" in favour of art as a social practice — construction rather than composition. Grew out of Suprematism (Malevich) and Tatlin's 1915 counter-reliefs. After 1917 it became aligned with Bolshevik ideology: artists were to be "engineers" producing useful objects, posters, textiles, theatre sets and books for the new Soviet state. Suppressed by Socialist Realism around 1934.

## Technique

- **Photomontage** — cut-up press photographs recombined for propaganda impact
- **Diagonal axis composition** — dynamic, off-kilter grids rather than classical symmetry
- Geometric abstraction: rectangles, circles, bars, arrows
- Restricted palette, almost always **red, black, and white** (sometimes cream or yellow)
- Sans-serif slab lettering, frequently set in wedges or running along diagonals
- Integration of typography and image as one structural field
- Printed via lithography or letterpress; book covers often two-colour

## Visual signatures for AI

- Red / black / white palette
- Hard-edged geometric shapes: circles, triangles, heavy bars
- Strong diagonal composition, often 30-45 degrees
- Photomontaged faces or workers' hands inside geometric frames
- Heavy sans-serif Cyrillic or Latin display type
- No atmospheric perspective — flat, graphic, declarative
