# Persian Miniature (Safavid / Timurid)

**Period:** 1400-1700, with peak at the Safavid courts of Tabriz, Qazvin, and Isfahan

## Overview

Persian miniature painting is a courtly book art developed in royal ketab-khaneh (book workshops) under Timurid and Safavid patronage. Images illustrate epic poetry — the *Shahnameh* of Ferdowsi, Nezami's *Khamsa*, the poems of Hafez and Sa'di — as well as historical chronicles and albums (*muraqqa*). It is a tradition of refined line, saturated colour, and symbolic rather than optical space, and remains a living practice today.

## Technique

- gouache and watercolour on sized paper, with gum arabic binder
- burnishing the paper with agate to produce a luminous surface
- pigments including [[lapis-lazuli-pigment|lapis lazuli]], malachite, vermilion, orpiment, and lead white
- [[gold-leaf-illumination|gold leaf]] for haloes, clouds, and border illumination (*tazhib*)
- tiny squirrel-hair brushes for fine linear detail
- [[rejection-of-perspective|non-linear perspective]] with elevated viewpoint

## Key masters and works

- [[behzad|Kamal ud-Din Behzad]] — Herat school, *Bustan of Sa'di*
- [[sultan-mohammed|Sultan Mohammed]] — *Shahnameh* of Shah Tahmasp
- [[reza-abbasi|Reza Abbasi]] — Isfahan school single-figure studies
- *Houghton Shahnameh* (c.1525-40), dispersed across Met Museum and others

## Visual signatures for AI

- flat planes of pure saturated colour; no atmospheric recession
- tilted ground plane with figures stacked vertically rather than receding
- ornate flowering meadows, geometric tilework, script cartouches
- elongated, slender figures with almond eyes and small mouths
- intricate textile patterns rendered without foreshortening
- ultramarine-blue skies with gold cloud bands
- compositional framing by architectural arches and poetic text panels
