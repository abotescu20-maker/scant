# Swiss Poster / International Typographic Style

**Period:** c. 1950-1970s

## Overview

A graphic design movement that emerged in Switzerland (centred on Zurich and Basel) in the 1950s and dominated professional graphic design worldwide through the 1960s-70s — hence the name International Typographic Style (ITS). Its goal was objective, legible, universal visual communication: "design as problem-solving." Corporate identity systems, signage, magazines (e.g. *Neue Grafik*), and exhibition posters were its main outputs.

## Technique

- **Grid systems** — underlying mathematical grid (Müller-Brockmann codified this in *Grid Systems in Graphic Design*, 1981)
- **Sans-serif typography**, ranged left / ragged right; Akzidenz-Grotesk, then Helvetica (1957) and Univers (1957)
- Flush-left asymmetric layout replaced centred axial composition
- Minimal decoration; no illustration unless functional
- **Halftone black-and-white photography** or pure geometric abstraction
- Restrained palette: often black + one accent (red, yellow, blue)
- Clear visual hierarchy driven by scale and weight, not ornament

## Visual signatures for AI

- Strict underlying grid visible in alignment of elements
- Huge Helvetica headline, tiny body type
- Single duotone photograph or one bold geometric shape
- Lots of white (negative) space
- Ragged-right paragraph blocks
- Accent colour limited to red or yellow on white / black
- Informational, corporate, almost clinical tone
