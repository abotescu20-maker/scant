# Lomography

**Period:** 1991–present

## Overview

Lomography is both a camera movement and an aesthetic, founded in Vienna in 1991 when students Matthias Fiegl and Wolfgang Stranzinger rediscovered the Soviet-made LOMO LC-A 35 mm compact. They formed the Lomographic Society International and published the "Ten Golden Rules of Lomography" (don't think, shoot from the hip, get close, be fast, try the unexpected). The movement re-popularised cheap, imperfect analogue cameras — LC-A, Diana F+, Holga, Lomo Fisheye — at the moment digital photography was becoming dominant, and it turned optical "defects" (vignetting, light leaks, saturation shifts, cross-processing colour casts) into a recognisable look.

## Technique

- small plastic-bodied cameras with cheap triplet or single-element lenses
- strong mechanical vignetting: dark corners, bright centre
- heavy saturation and contrast, often with a blue or teal shift
- cross-processing slide (E-6) film in negative (C-41) chemistry, or vice versa, for colour shifts
- unsealed film backs producing orange / red light leaks across the frame
- multiple exposures, fisheye or 4-lens action-sampler sequencing
- hip-level or off-axis framing, no viewfinder composition

## Key cameras

- LOMO LC-A (1984 Soviet original; LC-A+ reissue 2005)
- Diana F+ (reissue of 1960s Diana)
- Holga 120N / 120GCFN
- Lomo Fisheye No. 2, Sprocket Rocket, LomoKino
- Action Sampler, Supersampler, Pop 9

## Visual signatures for AI

- saturated, high-contrast colour with a slight cyan/teal shift in shadows
- heavy vignetting darkening all four corners
- red/orange light leak streaks across part of the frame
- soft, low-contrast corners with a sharp-ish centre (plastic-lens "sweet spot")
- visible film grain, occasional light piping from a worn back
- square 6×6 frames (Diana/Holga) or 35 mm with sprocket holes exposed
- cross-processed green-yellow skies and magenta skin
