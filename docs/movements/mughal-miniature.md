# Mughal Miniature Painting

**Period:** 1560-1800, at the imperial ateliers of Akbar, Jahangir, and Shah Jahan

## Overview

Mughal miniature painting emerged when Emperor Humayun invited Persian masters to India and Akbar (r.1556-1605) established the imperial *tasvir khana*. It fused [[persian-miniature|Persian miniature]] line with Indian colour and European naturalism arriving via Jesuit prints. The result is a distinct imperial idiom: crowded historical chronicles, sensitive portraiture, and precise natural-history studies. It remained a court art until Mughal decline and continues in revival workshops today.

## Technique

- opaque watercolour (gouache) on burnished paper
- [[fine-parallel-hatching|fine parallel hatching (*pardakht*)]] for skin and fabric modelling
- [[atmospheric-sky-gradation|horizon sky gradation]] absorbed from European oil painting
- team authorship — one artist drew the outline (*tarh*), a second coloured (*rangamizi*), a third did faces (*chihranama*)
- [[lapis-lazuli-pigment|lapis lazuli]], indigo, malachite, carmine from lac insects
- gold for borders, jewelry, and halo effects around emperors

## Key masters and works

- [[basawan|Basawan]] — Hindu master under Akbar, *Akbarnama*
- [[abd-al-samad|Khwaja Abd al-Samad]] — Persian-born founder of the Akbari studio
- [[daswanth|Daswanth]] — key illustrator of the *Razmnama*
- Mansur — Jahangir's natural-history painter (zebra, dodo, tulips)
- *Padshahnama* (Royal Library, Windsor)

## Visual signatures for AI

- more atmospheric depth than Persian — hazy blue-grey horizons
- densely populated durbar scenes with individualised portraits
- restrained palette with jewel-tone accents rather than uniform saturation
- European-derived shading on faces, but flat textile ornament
- precise observation of elephants, horses, birds, flowering plants
- profile portraiture with halo for emperor, three-quarter for courtiers
- minutely rendered carpets, marble inlay (*pietra dura*), jali screens
