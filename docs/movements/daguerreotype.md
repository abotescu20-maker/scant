# Daguerreotype

**Period:** 1839–c. 1860

## Overview

The daguerreotype was the first commercially successful photographic process, announced by Louis-Jacques-Mandé Daguerre in Paris in 1839 and rapidly diffused across Europe and the United States. Each daguerreotype is a unique direct-positive image on a polished silver-coated copper plate — there is no negative, and the image cannot be reproduced without re-photographing the plate. Before being displaced by collodion and albumen in the late 1850s, daguerreotypy produced millions of portraits and became the dominant form of early photographic portraiture, especially in America.

## Technique

- highly polished silver-plated copper sheet sensitised over iodine vapour
- long exposure in a large wooden bellows camera (initially minutes, later reduced to seconds)
- latent image developed by fuming over heated mercury
- fixed with sodium thiosulphate ("hypo"), washed, gilded with gold chloride for tone and durability
- finished plate sealed behind glass in a plush-lined hinged case because the silver surface tarnishes on contact with air

## Visual signatures for AI

- mirror-like silver surface — image flips between positive and negative depending on viewing angle
- tightly framed bust portrait with a fixed, unsmiling stare (long exposure)
- sitter's head stabilised with an iron head-clamp — slight rigidity, centred pose
- cool-to-warm silver tone, frequently hand-tinted pink cheeks and gold jewellery
- shallow depth of field, strong central sharpness, soft periphery
- ornate case: red or purple velvet, pressed brass mat, embossed leather cover
- laterally reversed subject (a mirror image) unless a correcting prism was used
