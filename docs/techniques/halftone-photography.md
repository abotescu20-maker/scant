# Halftone photography

## Technique

A reprographic process that converts continuous-tone photographs into a pattern of small black dots of varying size, so that the image can be printed with a single ink on a press. At normal viewing distance the dot pattern blends back into apparent grey tones; up close the dots are the defining texture.

Characteristic features:

- Regular dot grid (or line screen) — often 60, 85, 100 or 133 lines per inch
- Larger dots in shadows, smaller dots in highlights
- Moiré patterns can appear when two halftone screens overlap at incorrect angles
- Print reproduction in black-and-white newspapers, magazines, and posters from the 1880s onwards
- In [[swiss-poster-international-typographic-style|Swiss poster design]], a grainy halftone portrait is often the single pictorial element, paired with [[helvetica-typography|Helvetica]] headline and generous white space
- Pop Art (Lichtenstein, Warhol) and [[banksy-street-art|Street Art]] (Shepard Fairey's *Hope*) adopt the halftone dot as an aesthetic signature
