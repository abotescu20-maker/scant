# Raw material aesthetic

## Technique

Design principle of leaving materials in their as-worked state rather than applying concealing finishes. In Brutalist architecture this means exposed concrete with shuttering marks, unpainted blockwork, unlacquered metals, unstained timber. The Smithsons called this "as found" — buildings should honestly declare what they are made of and how they were built.

Visual markers:
- no skim-coat, no cladding, no false ceilings
- service runs (pipes, ducts, conduit) exposed on ceilings and walls
- mechanical systems expressed as forms — stair towers, lift shafts, ventilation stacks
- junctions between materials left plainly visible rather than masked by trim
- patina and weathering accepted as part of the design

This is an ethical position as much as an aesthetic one — honesty of construction elevated to a value. It also reflected post-war budget constraints that made concealment unaffordable.

## Practitioners

- Alison and Peter Smithson — Hunstanton School (1954), Economist Building (1964)
