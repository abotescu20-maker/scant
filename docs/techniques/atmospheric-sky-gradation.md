# Atmospheric Sky Gradation

## Overview

A sky rendered with a graduated transition — warm or pale at the horizon, deepening to a saturated blue at the zenith — adopted into [[mughal-miniature|Mughal miniature]] painting from European oil painting and engraving, which circulated at the court of Akbar via Jesuit missions from the 1580s. It is one of the principal features that distinguishes a Mughal sky from a [[persian-miniature|Persian]] sky, which is typically a flat lapis blue or gold.

## Method

- horizon band of pale pink, cream, or pale blue
- wet-over-dry gouache wash transitioning upward into deeper ultramarine or indigo
- sometimes a warm glow behind a hillside or dome to suggest time of day
- faint clouds either in gold (Persian inheritance) or in soft grey (European inheritance)
- paired with [[fine-parallel-hatching|parallel hatching]] to extend atmospheric modelling to landscape forms

## Why it matters for AI

A flat lapis sky signals Persian; a graded horizon signals Mughal. The difference is small on the page but decisive for style attribution. A model asked for "Mughal" that produces a flat Safavid sky has confused the two traditions.
