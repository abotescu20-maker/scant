# Plastic Lens

## Technique

Most toy and Lomo cameras use a single-element or two-element moulded plastic lens rather than a corrected multi-element glass lens. The choice is driven by cost, but its optical consequences are central to the [[lomography|Lomographic]] aesthetic:
- uncorrected spherical aberration — sharp at the centre, increasingly soft toward the edges
- strong [[vignetting]] (cos⁴ law amplified by small aperture and short flange)
- visible chromatic aberration: coloured fringes on high-contrast edges
- low contrast overall, rendering highlights slightly milky
- lens flare from internal reflections between plastic surfaces
- distortion, usually pronounced barrel distortion

Cameras emblematic of the look include the Holga 120N (60 mm f/8 plastic meniscus), the Diana F+ (75 mm f/11), and the Lomo LC-A — though the LC-A's Minitar-1 is actually a glass lens, its other cheap construction features produce a closely related look.
