# Stencil Master

## Technique

The physical carrier of the image in a Risograph. When a page is sent to the Riso, a thermal head burns tiny holes through a thin polymer-and-rice-paper sheet — the "master" — one master per spot-colour layer. The master is automatically wrapped around a cylindrical ink drum. As the drum rotates at high speed, soy-based ink is forced outwards through the burned holes and transferred onto the paper passing underneath.

Key consequences of the stencil-master architecture:
- only one colour can print per pass — multi-colour jobs need multiple passes, one drum swap per colour
- each master is disposable and takes only a few seconds to generate, which is why Riso is economical at 20–500 copies but not at 5
- because the master is a perforated stencil, tones cannot be printed continuously: greys and gradients must be simulated with [[halftone-moire|halftones]]
- ink sits on top of uncoated paper rather than being absorbed, so solids show slight unevenness and show-through on the reverse
- the first few copies of each run have inconsistent ink flow — printers typically discard 2–5 warm-up sheets

This close kinship with screen-printing — stencil + ink + substrate — is why the Risograph inherits so much of the visual DNA of mid-20th-century silkscreen.
