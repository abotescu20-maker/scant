# Helvetica typography

## Technique

Helvetica is a neo-grotesque sans-serif typeface designed by Max Miedinger and Eduard Hoffmann at the Haas Type Foundry in Münchenstein, Switzerland, released in 1957 as *Neue Haas Grotesk* and renamed *Helvetica* in 1960 for international export. It became the emblematic face of the [[swiss-poster-international-typographic-style|International Typographic Style]] and of post-war corporate identity.

Characteristic features:

- Even stroke weight with minimal contrast
- Tight, compact letterforms that sit comfortably on a [[grid-system|grid]]
- Horizontal terminals on c, e, s, r — cut flat
- Large x-height; legible at small sizes
- Designed to look "neutral" / "objective" — no decorative flourishes
- Typically set flush-left ragged-right in Swiss practice, not justified

## Use conventions

- Headline very large, body text very small — the jump in scale creates hierarchy
- Single weight family (Regular, Bold) is preferred over decorative mixing
- Works with [[halftone-photography|halftone photography]] and pure geometry, rarely with ornament
- Univers (Adrian Frutiger, 1957) is the functionally equivalent sister face
