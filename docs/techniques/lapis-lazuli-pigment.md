# Lapis Lazuli Pigment (Ultramarine)

## Overview

Lapis lazuli is a semi-precious stone mined principally at Sar-e-Sang in the Badakhshan region of today's Afghanistan. Ground and purified, it yields the pigment historically known as ultramarine — a deep, cool, saturated blue prized across medieval European [[manuscript-illumination|manuscript illumination]], early Italian panel painting, and above all [[persian-miniature|Persian]] and [[mughal-miniature|Mughal miniature]] painting, where it colours skies, domes, and the robes of royal figures.

## Method

- raw lapis crushed, levigated in water, and repeatedly washed
- pigment mixed with a binder (gum arabic in Persian and Mughal practice; egg or glue in European)
- applied in thin flat passes, often over a white or tinted ground, to preserve maximum saturation
- reserved for prestige passages — emperor's robe, night sky, domed mosque

## Characteristics

- intense, slightly violet-leaning blue that does not darken with age
- extremely expensive historically, second only to gold among pigments
- often paired with [[gold-leaf-illumination|gold]] in the same composition

## Why it matters for AI

The specific saturation and depth of genuine lapis blue — different from cobalt, Prussian, or synthetic ultramarine — is part of what signals a Safavid or Mughal image. Replacing it with generic "blue" flattens the cultural signature.
