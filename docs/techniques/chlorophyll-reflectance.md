# Chlorophyll Reflectance

## Technique

The physical mechanism that gives colour-infrared photography its unmistakable look. Living green leaves absorb most visible red and blue light (for photosynthesis) but reflect visible green weakly and near-infrared (roughly 700–1100 nm) very strongly — in fact more strongly than any other common outdoor surface.

To the eye, two green surfaces — real foliage and a green-painted wall — look almost identical. To an IR film or sensor they are radically different: the leaf blazes bright in the IR channel, the painted wall stays dark. On false-colour IR film, where IR is mapped to red, the leaf therefore renders as saturated pink / magenta / crimson while the paint stays green or grey.

This is also why colour infrared was developed for the military: it reveals camouflage nets and dead foliage against living forest. In contemporary art use (see [[richard-mosse|Richard Mosse]]) the same physics produces the dreamlike magenta jungle landscapes of *Infra* and *The Enclave*.

Dead or stressed vegetation, having little active chlorophyll, shows much lower IR reflectance and reverts to muddy brown or cyan — useful for vegetation-health mapping.
