# Board-formed concrete

## Technique

Cast-in-place reinforced concrete poured against formwork made of rough-sawn timber planks. When the formwork is struck, the concrete surface carries a permanent negative impression of the wood grain, plank joints, and nail holes. The result is a textured monolithic surface that reads as simultaneously raw and refined.

Key visual features:
- horizontal or vertical plank lines depending on formwork orientation
- visible "fins" at plank butt-joints where cement paste bled between boards
- grain texture transferred from the timber
- tie-rod holes left exposed in a regular grid (often plugged or left open)
- colour variation across pours recording construction sequence

The technique was elevated to aesthetic principle by [[le-corbusier|Le Corbusier]] at the Unité d'Habitation (Marseille, 1952) where he used the French term *béton brut* — raw concrete — which gave [[brutalism|Brutalism]] its name.

## Practitioners

- [[le-corbusier|Le Corbusier]] — Unité d'Habitation, Ronchamp, Chandigarh
- Tadao Andō — smoother successor (steel forms, but philosophically related)
- Louis Kahn — Salk Institute walls (1965)
- [[marcel-breuer|Marcel Breuer]] — Saint John's Abbey (1961)
