# Vignetting

## Technique

Vignetting is the progressive darkening of an image toward its corners. It has several optical and mechanical causes:
- natural falloff: the cos⁴ law — off-axis rays travel farther and strike the film at an angle, reducing illumination
- mechanical vignetting: the lens barrel, lens hood or filter ring physically blocks off-axis rays
- pixel vignetting (digital): microlenses on the sensor accept less light at steep angles

In precision photography vignetting is usually corrected. In [[lomography|Lomographic]] practice it is embraced. Cheap plastic cameras like the Holga and Lomo LC-A have small lenses and short flange-distances, so their corners fall off strongly — by 1.5 to 2 stops at the edges. This produces a bright-centred, dark-cornered frame that visually frames the subject and gives the whole image a "tunnel" feeling.

Visual markers:
- roughly circular dark halo, strongest at the four corners
- smooth radial gradient from a bright centre
- exaggerated by wide apertures and wide-angle or plastic lenses
- often combined with centre-weighted focus so the corners are also soft
