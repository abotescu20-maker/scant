# Mercury Vapor Development

## Technique

The signature development step of the daguerreotype. An exposed silver-iodide plate is suspended face-down over a cup of heated mercury. Mercury vapour adheres preferentially to the parts of the plate that have been struck by light, forming a fine lattice of mercury-silver amalgam particles. These tiny bright particles make up the image's highlights; unexposed areas remain dark, mirror-like silver.

The process explains several classic daguerreotype characteristics:
- very fine detail, because each amalgam particle is microscopic
- the peculiar positive/negative flip — when the plate is tilted away from a light source, highlights go dark, because they are scattering rather than reflecting
- high sensitivity to scratching: the amalgam sits loosely on the polished silver
- a known occupational-health hazard for nineteenth-century studio operators

Daguerre's breakthrough of 1839 was accidental: a long-exposed plate left in a cupboard with spilled mercury produced a visible image overnight.
