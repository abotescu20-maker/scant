# Cross-Processing

## Technique

Developing one type of colour film in the chemistry intended for another — most commonly processing E-6 colour-reversal (slide) film in C-41 negative chemistry, or vice versa. The dye couplers in each film are calibrated for a specific process; when they meet the wrong chemistry, contrast and colour balance shift in dramatic, semi-predictable ways.

Typical results of E-6-in-C-41:
- strongly increased contrast and saturation
- green-yellow casts in highlights, cyan / magenta casts in shadows
- skin tones slide toward green or yellow
- colour shifts vary from film to film — Fuji Sensia, Kodak Elitechrome and Agfa Precisa each produce different palettes

Cross-processing became a popular look with the [[lomography|Lomographic]] movement in the 1990s and with editorial / fashion photography of the early 2000s, partly because it pushed analogue film toward a more "graphic", less naturalistic palette at the moment digital was arriving.

## Practitioners

- Lomographic Society International (popularisation)
- David LaChapelle, Jürgen Teller (editorial use)
- countless amateur and zine photographers
