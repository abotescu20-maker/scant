# Monumental scale

## Technique

Deliberate amplification of architectural dimensions so that elements dwarf the human figure, conveying civic importance, institutional permanence, or sculptural grandeur. In Brutalism, this is achieved not by ornamentation but by sheer mass of concrete volume.

Compositional features:
- broad unbroken vertical and horizontal planes spanning 10-30+ metres
- oversized pilotis (columns) that could be tree-trunk thick
- deep-set window recesses creating shadow apertures rather than ribbon glazing
- projecting brise-soleil and cantilevers that read from long distance
- plazas and podiums that enforce low approach sightlines
- human figure reduced to ~1/10 of the visible façade in frontal photography

Antecedents include Étienne-Louis Boullée's visionary drawings (c. 1780s), Albert Speer's fascist monumentalism (rejected politically but a formal reference), and Hugh Ferriss's *Metropolis of Tomorrow* (1929).

## Practitioners

- [[le-corbusier|Le Corbusier]] — Chandigarh Capitol Complex, Secretariat (1958)
- Oscar Niemeyer — Brasília government buildings (1957-60, concrete monumentalism)
- Paul Rudolph — Orange County Government Center (1967)
- Kenzō Tange — Shizuoka Press and Broadcasting Center (1967)
- [[ernoe-goldfinger|Ernő Goldfinger]] — Trellick Tower (1972)
