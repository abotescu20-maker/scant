# False-Color Emulsion

## Technique

The defining structural idea of Kodak Aerochrome and later colour-infrared films. A normal colour reversal film has three emulsion layers sensitised to blue, green and red. In a false-colour IR film the blue-sensitive layer is replaced by one sensitised to near-infrared (roughly 700–900 nm), so the three recorded channels are green, red and near-IR.

On processing, the three layers are coupled to conventional yellow, magenta and cyan dyes — but *shifted by one channel*. The result is a systematic, reproducible colour translation:
- near-IR reflectance → rendered red
- visible red reflectance → rendered green
- visible green reflectance → rendered blue

Because the film is still sensitive to blue light, a yellow (minus-blue) filter must be used over the lens; otherwise blue scatter fogs all three layers. This rule-based colour shift is what makes IR photographs predictable rather than random: healthy leaves always go pink, water always goes black.
