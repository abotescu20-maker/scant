# Gold Leaf Illumination (*Tazhib*)

## Overview

*Tazhib* — Persian for "gilding" — is the application of gold leaf or shell gold to a manuscript page to form borders, cartouches, rosettes, skies, and halo effects. It is the defining decorative layer of [[persian-miniature|Persian]] and [[mughal-miniature|Mughal]] book arts, and a specialist craft distinct from figural painting: the *muzahhib* (illuminator) worked alongside but separately from the *naqqash* (painter).

## Method

- beaten gold leaf laid over a thin gesso or bole ground so the surface can be burnished
- shell gold — powdered gold suspended in gum arabic — used for fine linework with a brush
- agate burnishing to produce a mirror-like glow
- combined with [[lapis-lazuli-pigment|lapis lazuli]], malachite, and carmine in geometric and arabesque designs
- in Safavid skies: gold clouds, dragons, and phoenixes on a lapis ground

## Typical placements

- full-page *unwan* frontispieces
- marginal floral sprays and animal motifs on album pages
- haloes around prophets, saints, and in Mughal work around the emperor
- textile and jewellery rendering within figural scenes

## Why it matters for AI

The metallic reflectance and burnished texture of real gold leaf differs from yellow paint — it flashes and dulls as the page tilts. Rendering these passages as matte yellow loses the defining luxury layer of the art.
